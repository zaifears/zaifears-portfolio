## 1. THE INDUSTRY BACKDROP

Bangladesh's fintech ecosystem has matured into one of the most active in South Asia. Mobile financial services anchor the digital payment rails. October 2025 alone saw 678.63 million MFS transactions worth BDT 1.58 trillion across the country, up 5.3 percent in volume and 2.82 percent in value over September. Cash out remains the largest single transaction type by count, while P2P remains the largest by value. The composition tells the real story: A market that has scaled enormously yet still leans heavily on cash conversion rather than digital loop usage.

The country's MFS providers collectively report more than 215 million registered accounts and roughly 9 crore active accounts that have transacted in the last three months. The central bank forecasts annual MFS transaction value above BDT 25 lakh crore by year end 2025. This volume conceals a more complicated reality. Active accounts represent less than 40 percent of registered accounts. Cash continues to dominate the wider financial system, accounting for over 65 percent of total transaction value as of October 2025.

| MFS transaction type | Number of transactions (million) | Transaction value (BDT billion) |
|---|---|---|
| P2P (person to person) | 134.26 | 476.93 |
| Cash in | 142.55 | 413.17 |
| Cash out | 174.56 | 372.23 |
| **Total MFS (October 2025)** | **678.63** | **1,580 (1.58 trillion)** |

The country has roughly 1.2 crore micro, small, and medium enterprises, yet more than 60 percent of micro enterprises cannot access formal credit. Those that do often pay between 24 and 40 percent in interest to informal lenders. This is the gap that alternative credit scoring fintechs were built to close. The opportunity is large and largely untouched. A company sitting on credit scoring infrastructure today is not in a saturated market. It is in the earliest phase of a market that is just beginning to monetise at scale.

Bangladesh Bank licenses two types of digital financial entities under the Bangladesh Payment and Settlement Systems Regulations 2014. Payment Service Providers offer wallet, e money issuance, and value added services including BNPL facilitation with partner lenders and fraud scoring. Payment System Operators run switching, scheme, or clearing infrastructure. MFS specifically is bank led under the 2018 MFS Regulations, requiring a scheduled bank, financial institution, or government entity to hold majority equity in any operating subsidiary. The Regulatory FinTech Facilitation Office, established in October 2019, runs the central bank's sandbox programme for new propositions.

The capital that built this ecosystem was overwhelmingly foreign. Sovereign wealth funds, growth equity from East Asia, and global development finance institutions wrote most of the major cheques. The largest domestic MFS provider's valuation moved from roughly BDT 7,500 crore in 2018 to BDT 17,000 crore in 2021 on the back of one foreign investor's entry. That capital came with terms. Multi year exit clauses, drag along rights, and qualified IPO definitions sit in nearly every term sheet signed since 2018.

Bangladesh Bank has grown more explicit about the regulatory perimeter as exits begin to surface. Change of beneficial control of a licensed institution triggers a fit and proper review that examines beneficial ownership two levels up the ownership chain. The 49 percent foreign holding cap on licensed payment institutions under the Foreign Exchange Regulation Act 1947 remains operative. The Cyber Security Act 2023 and the draft data protection bill, expected to pass within the case period, govern cross border data flows. Together these create a tighter exit funnel than existed when the original capital came in.

## 2. THE CLAUSE

Ten years ago, Aurin Technologies signed the following clause with its lead investor, the Mehran Asia Growth Fund (MAGF). Five years have passed. Five years remain before the clause becomes operative and MAGF can compel a Qualified Exit Event. The committee is not in the emergency window yet. You are in the planning window, and how you use the next five years determines whether the exit is on your terms or MAGF's.

---

*If, within ten years of the Investment Closing Date, the Company has not undergone a Qualified Exit Event (defined as a trade sale, merger, or Qualified IPO), MAGF shall have the right to compel the Company to initiate IPO proceedings on a recognized stock exchange, either the Dhaka Stock Exchange, the Chittagong Stock Exchange, or an internationally recognized exchange mutually agreed upon, within 18 months of exercising this right. A Qualified IPO is defined as a public offering that implies a minimum pre money valuation of 2.5 times MAGF's entry valuation (i.e. minimum BDT 1,607 crore) and results in MAGF being able to liquidate at least 40 percent of its shareholding either through secondary offering on IPO day or through a lock up expiry sale within 12 months of listing. Should the Company fail to meet IPO readiness criteria within the compelled timeline, MAGF reserves the right to initiate a drag along sale of the entire company at fair market value determined by an independent valuer.*

---

Read the clause carefully. The IPO is the mechanism, not the objective. MAGF's actual contractual goal is liquidity at a 2.5x minimum. Any alternative path that delivers MAGF that financial outcome is, in principle, negotiable. The drag along is the regulator of last resort, designed to make refusal expensive.

You are the investment committee advising the founders of Aurin Technologies. Five years have elapsed since MAGF's investment closed. Five years remain on the clause. You will engineer the exit, choose the levers, and build the contingency for what happens when the plan does not work.

## 4. THE COMPANY: AURIN TECHNOLOGIES

Aurin Technologies builds the credit infrastructure that licensed lenders depend on to serve borrowers who have no formal credit history. Its alternative credit scoring engine, trained on mobile recharge frequency, utility payment patterns, MFS transaction footprints, and merchant cash flow signals, is what makes lending to underbanked Bangladeshis commercially viable for partner banks and microfinance institutions. Aurin holds a Bangladesh Bank PSP license and operates a small lending arm of its own as live proof that the engine performs.

Aurin's BDT 180 crore monthly transaction volume sits inside a national MFS market that just crossed BDT 1.58 trillion in monthly volume. The headroom is structural, not theoretical. Less than 0.012 percent of the national MFS volume currently runs through Aurin's underwriting layer. Most micro and small enterprise credit demand in the country remains unaddressed. The runway exists if the company can find the right route to reach it.

### Aurin Technologies — Snapshot at year five

| Aurin Technologies | Snapshot at year five |
|---|---|
| **Product** | Alternative credit scoring engine for the underbanked. Trained on mobile recharge patterns, utility payments, MFS transaction footprints, and merchant behaviour. Operates a small lending arm as proof of concept for the engine. |
| **Customers** | 400,000 active merchant accounts in Tier 2 and Tier 3 cities. Monthly transaction volume of BDT 180 crore on a national MFS base where October 2025 industry volume crossed BDT 1.58 trillion. Two consecutive years of operating profitability. |
| **Channel partners** | Two scheduled commercial banks and one NBFI consume Aurin's scoring API for their digital lending programmes. Aurin also has a working pilot with an MFI network. |
| **Regulatory standing** | Holds a Bangladesh Bank Payment Service Provider license under the Bangladesh Payment and Settlement Systems Regulations 2014. Reports under the AML Act 2012 to BFIU. Engages with Bangladesh Bank's Regulatory FinTech Facilitation Office (RFFO) for sandbox guidance. |
| **Founders** | Two cofounders hold 61 percent combined. Both founders are domain operators, not financiers. |

### The two growth levers in front of the founders

Aurin's path to a higher valuation depends on what the founders do with the next five years. Two distinct expansion theses are on the table, and the committee will need to decide whether to pursue one, the other, both in parallel, both in sequence, or neither. The case does not prescribe the answer.

#### Lever One: Deeper domestic penetration

Aurin currently powers credit scoring for two scheduled commercial banks and one NBFI. The Bangladesh Bank's circular on digital lending encourages scheduled banks to develop end to end digital loan products. There are 61 scheduled banks and roughly 35 active NBFIs in the country. Onboarding a meaningful subset of these institutions as paying API customers would multiply Aurin's addressable base from 400,000 directly served merchants to a potential 4 to 6 million end borrowers reached through banking channel partnerships. Recurring API revenue would compound. The thesis is that Aurin moves from being a fintech lender to being credit infrastructure.

#### Lever Two: Adjacent product expansion

Aurin's scoring engine is, at its core, a probability model trained on Bangladeshi behavioural data. The same engine, repurposed, could power scoring for SME working capital lending, agricultural credit scoring through MFI networks, and insurance underwriting for the underbanked segment. Each adjacent product adds a revenue stream without requiring a new customer base. The thesis is that Aurin moves from being a single product company to being a credit data platform.

Each lever, pursued credibly, lifts Aurin's projected valuation at year ten meaningfully above today's BDT 2,000 crore implied valuation. Pursued together they lift it further. Pursued at the wrong pace they exhaust management bandwidth and stall execution. The committee must defend its choice.

## 5. SCENARIO A: BEST CASE — THE PROFITABLE PATIENCE TRAP

This is the world in which Aurin executes well over the next five years. The company remains profitable. Both growth levers are pursued in some combination.

The trap is that MAGF can compel exit at year ten. If the IPO happens at year ten, the company lists before its full uplift has been priced in. If the structured secondary sale happens at year ten, MAGF gets its liquidity but Aurin loses the option to wait.

The committee will need to satisfy MAGF's liquidity obligation without surrendering the upside the next five years are likely to produce. Several deal structures, capital sources, and timing choices are available. The judgement lies in which combination protects both sides of the cap table.

---

## 6. SCENARIO B: WORST CASE — THE PROFITABILITY PRESSURE

This is the world in which the next five years do not break the way the founders hoped. Headline inflation runs above 9 percent through year seven. Lending defaults in Aurin's small in house book rise from 4 percent to 8 percent. The two scheduled banks that consume Aurin's API renegotiate their commercials downward. Operating profit compresses to break even. The Series C round Aurin was preparing for year three slipped to year four and closed at a flat valuation. The Singapore based co investor has aligned with MAGF in pushing for the exit at year ten.

Several domestic vehicles are conceivable here, individually or in combination. The committee will also need to consider whether the company is more valuable as a whole or as a sum of its parts. Founder protection in any forced restructuring should be designed in, not assumed.

## 7. YOUR SUBMISSION

Your submission must address seven tasks. Task 1 is the case understanding deliverable, intended to demonstrate to the judging panel that your team has correctly internalised the problem before solving it. Tasks 2 through 7 are the substantive deliverables. The marking weights for each component are set out in Section 8.

### Task 1: Case understanding and business summary

Open your presentation by summarising the case in your own words. The business, the clause, the timeline, the stakeholders, the constraints, and what you understand the question to be asking. This task is marked. It signals to the panel whether you have read the case carefully before solving it. The strongest submissions will compress the case into three to four slides without losing material accuracy.

### Task 2: Financial defence and modelling

Build the financial model that supports your exit recommendation. Required components include a DCF valuation of Aurin under both scenarios, MOIC and IRR for MAGF under each row of each priority matrix, NPV of each option from MAGF's perspective, the founders' retained equity value, and the cost of each option in transaction fees, regulatory overhead, and management time. State assumptions openly. The panel rewards defensibility over optimism. Excel file submission is mandatory.

### Task 3: Exit Strategy and Priority matrices

Produce two matrices. The first applies to the best case scenario in Section 5. The second applies to the worst case scenario in Section 6.

Each row carries an Action and a Rationale, supported by financial and regulatory reasoning. In the best case, defend your choice on the two growth levers. In the worst case, the available restructuring paths are starting points, not the answer. Original combinations and creative structures will be rewarded if they are feasible and defensible.

For each priority row in both matrices, the exit strategy must be presented in two layers.

The first is the qualitative layer. Describe the deal structure: what mechanism is being used (secondary sale, IPO, structured stake transfer, scheme of arrangement, or others), who the counterparty is, what the governance and operational terms look like, and why this structure is the right fit for Aurin's regulatory standing and founder position.

The second is the quantitative layer. Show the numbers that underpin the deal. This must include the implied valuation at the time of execution, MAGF's realised proceeds, the percentage of the 40 percent liquidation obligation met, the consideration split (e.g., cash, equity rollover, deferred payment, and earnouts if applicable); and any residual stake that MAGF retains after the transaction. Where the exit is phased, show the value at each phase.

A recommendation that cannot be supported by both layers will be treated as incomplete regardless of its strategic logic.

### Task 4: Regulatory and legal analysis

For each priority row in each matrix, map the approvals required and the realistic timeline. Bangladesh Bank change of beneficial control under the Payment and Settlement Systems Regulations 2014, fit and proper review, Foreign Exchange Regulation Act 1947 clearance where applicable, BIDA registration, Competition Commission of Bangladesh notification under the Competition Act 2012, BFIU and AML Act 2012 obligations, Cyber Security Act 2023 compliance, and Companies Act 1994 mechanics where a scheme of arrangement applies. Tie each filing to the five year planning window. Identify the choke points.

### Task 5: Risk assessment and contingency framework

Identify the operational, financial, competitive, macroeconomic, and execution risks that could destabilise your exit plan in years six through ten. For each, propose a specific mitigation or contingency response. The contingency framework is not the same as the worst case matrix. The matrix answers what happens if the plan does not deliver. The contingency framework answers what early warning signals you would monitor and what concrete actions you would take when they fire.

### Task 6: Deal execution and stakeholder coordination

Show how your priority matrix lands across the five stakeholders whose interests must be reconciled. MAGF, which must receive its 2.5x minimum and 40 percent liquidity. The founders, who must either retain meaningful upside or face the drag along. Bangladesh Bank, which must approve any change of control. The customers, who must retain trust through any restructuring. The employees, whose continuity matters for the company's operating reality during transition. Show the sequencing, the dependencies, and the coordination mechanics across these five constituencies.

### Task 7: Presentation and Q and A

The final round is a verbal defence as much as a written submission. Your slides should tell a coherent story end to end, your speakers should command the material, and your responses to panel questions should hold up to challenge. Submissions that look strong on paper but fall apart in Q and A will be marked accordingly.

### Bonus: Creativity points

Submissions that introduce credible, original structural ideas, deal mechanics, or stakeholder arrangements that are not present in the case material and that survive a feasibility and regulatory test will receive bonus marks. Creativity for its own sake is not rewarded. Creativity that is defendable under panel questioning is. The bonus is capped at 5 marks.
